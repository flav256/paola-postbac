[1:30-2:00] — Why I want Hotelschool

2. Essay-related questions — Resilience theme

The interviewer has read your essay about the boat party cancellation. Expect follow-up questions on it. Your essay positions you as calm under pressure, solutions-oriented, and honest in communication. Build on this.

"I learned that my first instinct under pressure is to rush, but that taking one extra day to compare options led to a better outcome. I also discovered that honest, calm communication builds more trust than pretending nothing went wrong. This experience showed me that I handle stress best when I focus on solutions rather than the problem itself — a strength I want to develop further in hospitality, where unexpected changes are part of every day."

Likely follow-up questions on the essay :

Q1. "Tell me more about the moment you realised the venue had cancelled. What was your very first reaction?"
"Honestly, my very first reaction was a wave of panic. Fifty people had already paid, the date was set, and a lot of trust was on the line. But within a few minutes I realised that panic wouldn't help — and that my job as the coordinator was to be the calm centre of the group, not to spread the stress. So I gave myself one hour to process it, talked to my closest team-mates so they wouldn't feel alone with the news, and then sat down with a list of every boat company in Abu Dhabi. That was the moment I shifted from problem mode to solution mode."
Q2. "You write that your instinct is to rush. How did you stop yourself from rushing?"
"By forcing myself to write down the criteria first — capacity, price, date availability, location — before contacting anyone. I realised that on day one, I almost booked the first option that said yes, because I just wanted the problem to disappear. But I had a quick conversation with my mother that evening, and she pointed out that booking quickly might cost us more than waiting one more day. That was the lesson — patience under pressure isn't passivity, it's strategy."
Q3. "How did you communicate the change to the 50 classmates?"
"I drafted one clear, honest message — explaining what happened, what I had done, and what the new arrangement was. I avoided over-apologising because I didn't want to sound like I had lost control. Instead I positioned the new venue positively : the deck was actually better, the route nicer. I think honesty plus a positive framing is what worked. Several classmates told me afterwards that they hadn't even realised there had been an issue, which I took as the best possible feedback."
Q4. "Hospitality involves crisis management every day. Give me another example where you stayed calm under pressure."
"During my Navy internship in Abu Dhabi last year, we did crisis simulation exercises. We had to respond to a fire alarm scenario in a confined space, with strict protocols. What I learned there was that the people who panic aren't the ones who don't know what to do — they're the ones who try to do everything at once. The officers who impressed me most were the calmest. They focused on one task at a time. I try to apply that mindset now : when something goes wrong, pick the one thing that matters most in the next 10 minutes."
Q5. "Is there anything you would do differently if you had to organise the boat party again?"
"Yes — I would have built a Plan B from the start. We had booked the venue six weeks in advance and assumed it was confirmed. I now believe that for any event, you need a documented backup, even if you never use it. In hospitality, this is actually standard practice : double bookings, alternative suppliers, weather plans. It's something I want to learn properly during my studies."
Q6. "What does resilience mean to you?"
"For me, resilience isn't about not feeling stressed — it's about what you do with the stress. It's the ability to absorb a shock, take a breath, and come back to the task. I think it's also about not being too proud to ask for help. During the boat party crisis, I asked my mother and one of my classmates for advice, and that made all the difference. Resilience is a team sport, even when you're the one leading."

4. The luxury angle — Veblen, the experience economy, and Paola's Grand Oral

This is your differentiator. Most candidates talk about "loving hotels". You can talk about how the entire industry is shifting.

The intellectual frame : The luxury industry is moving from goods to experiences. This is a major shift documented by economists and observed across every luxury segment — from hotels (Aman, Six Senses) to fashion (LVMH experiential retail) to fine dining (Noma, El Celler de Can Roca).

Key references to drop naturally

Thorstein Veblen — The Theory of the Leisure Class (1899)
Veblen introduced the concept of "conspicuous consumption" — the idea that the wealthy use visible goods to display status. For over a century, luxury was about the object : the Hermès bag, the Patek Philippe watch, the suite at the Ritz.

What Paola can say : "Veblen described luxury as about visible objects that signal status. But today's customers — especially younger generations — are less interested in showing wealth than in collecting transformative experiences. The bag matters less than the story behind it."
Pine & Gilmore — The Experience Economy (1999)
This is the academic frame for the shift. Pine and Gilmore argue that economic value has evolved through 4 stages : commodities → goods → services → experiences. Hospitality sits in stage 4 — and luxury hospitality is the purest expression of it.

What Paola can say : "Pine and Gilmore described the shift toward an experience economy. In hospitality, this means the room is just the container — the real product is the memory the guest takes home."

Concrete current examples to mention

Personal experience — Paola has been to these (highest value, mention first) :

  • The Chedi Sharjah (UAE) — "I visited The Chedi in Sharjah and was struck by how the whole property is designed as a single coherent experience — the architecture, the silence, the way staff move. It's not selling rooms, it's selling a state of mind. That's exactly the shift from goods to experiences in action."
  • Emirates Palace, Abu Dhabi — "I've been to the Emirates Palace, and what impressed me most wasn't the gold or the marble — it was the door staff who remembered my mother's name from a previous visit, six months apart. That level of recognition is what turns a hotel stay into a memory. It's the experience economy in its purest form."

Global benchmarks (use sparingly) :

  • Aman Resorts — sells "transformative experiences" not rooms. Each property is a destination.
  • Six Senses — wellness-led, sustainability-driven luxury.
  • LVMH — opened its largest store at Dubai Mall : 4 floors, not a shop but a "destination experience".
  • The Ritz Paris — after renovation, marketed not as "rooms" but as "the Paris of Hemingway and Coco Chanel".
  • Mandarin Oriental Bangkok — famous for its handwritten welcome notes, butler service, and Author's Lounge.
Strategic tip : Always lead with The Chedi Sharjah or Emirates Palace. Personal experience trumps theoretical knowledge — the jury will trust you more when you describe what you saw with your own eyes. Save Aman / Six Senses / Bangkok references for when you want to demonstrate broader industry literacy.

6. Hospitality industry — Current topics

The jury may ask about industry trends. Be ready with 3-4 talking points :

Sustainability in luxury hospitality
Luxury and sustainability are no longer opposites. Six Senses, 1 Hotels, and many independents have built their brand on sustainability. The new challenge : how do you create lavish experiences with minimal environmental impact? UN SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption) is now baked into hotel design.
AI and the future of guest service
AI is transforming operations — chatbots, dynamic pricing, predictive maintenance. But in luxury, the question is different : can AI ever replace the warmth of human service? My view : AI should free humans to do what AI can't — read emotions, anticipate needs, surprise guests.
The rise of bleisure and remote work
The line between business and leisure travel is fading. Hotels now design "live-work" spaces, longer-stay packages, co-working in the lobby. This is reshaping room design and F&B services.
Asia and Middle East as new luxury frontiers
Growing up in Abu Dhabi, I've seen how Asia and the Gulf are reshaping the luxury landscape. The Saudi Vision 2030, NEOM, the Red Sea Project — these aren't just hotels, they're new cities for hospitality. The next generation of luxury brands may not be European.

Q4. What does excellent guest experience mean to you?
"It means making someone feel seen. Not just served — seen. When I organised the senior dinner, I noticed that one of my classmates was vegetarian, and I added a vegetarian option without making a big deal of it. He thanked me three times. That's hospitality : noticing the small things before being asked."
Q5. What hotel experience has impressed you the most?
"Last summer I had brunch at the Emirates Palace in Abu Dhabi. What impressed me wasn't the gold or the marble — it was the door staff who remembered my mother's name from a previous visit. Six months apart, and they still remembered. That level of attention is what I want to learn to deliver."
Q6. Describe a time you worked in a team.
"For the three graduation events, I led a team of 6 classmates. We were friends, not colleagues, which made delegation tricky. I learned that you can't lead friends by giving orders — you have to convince them. So I made the budget visible to everyone, shared the to-do list publicly, and asked each person what they enjoyed doing most. Once people owned a piece of the project, the energy shifted completely."
Q7. What is your biggest weakness?
"I tend to want to control too much, because I care a lot about the outcome. During the event coordination, I sometimes did things myself instead of trusting my team. I've been working on this by setting clearer boundaries between what I own and what I delegate. It's a work in progress."
Q8. What is your biggest strength?
"Adaptability. I've moved between 5 countries, attended schools in 2 languages, and adapted to each one. In hospitality, where you serve guests from 50 countries in the same day, that's not a soft skill — it's a survival skill."

See section 5.
Q10. How do you handle stress?
"By breaking the problem into small concrete actions. During the boat party crisis, I made a list of every boat company in Abu Dhabi and called them one by one. Q14. What would you contribute to the Hotelschool community?
"Energy and event experience. I'd love to get involved in the student associations and contribute to organising events for the school. My interior design interest and my coordination skills could be useful there. I'd also bring a perspective from outside Europe — the Gulf hospitality market, which is reshaping the industry."
Q15. Do you have any questions for us?
Always ask 2. Lead with the first one (most powerful — projects you into the school) :
  • "Assuming a positive outcome to this interview, and that I'll have the privilege of joining Hotelschool in September — what would be your advice to make the most of the first year and excel? What are the keys to success at Hotelschool?"
  • "How do students typically choose between specialisation and pre-master in Year 4? Is there guidance during Year 3?"
  • "What kind of luxury hotel groups do students typically intern with in Year 2?"
  • "What's the alumni community like in the Middle East and Asia?"
Why the first question is strong : It signals confidence (you assume you'll be admitted), forward-looking commitment, humility (you're already thinking about how to deserve your place), and respect (you're asking for the interviewer's personal advice — they'll enjoy answering). It also gives them a glimpse of you as a future student, not just a candidate.

8. Closing statement (1 minute)

You will be asked to make a final 1-minute closing statement. Prepare it exactly.

"Thank you for this conversation. What I want to leave you with is this :

I'm not applying to Hotelschool because I love staying in hotels. I'm applying because I've already organised events for 230+ people, I've already handled a real logistics crisis, I've already lived in 5 countries — and through all of that, I've found that what I love most is designing moments for other people. That's hospitality.

Hotelschool gives me what I can't get on my own : the rigour, the Skotel experience, the alumni network, and a 4-year apprenticeship in an industry I already feel at home in.

Delivery : Slow down. Make eye contact with the camera. Don't smile too much — close on a calm, confident note. Pause at the end. Don't fill the silence.

9. Dress, tech, mindset — Day-of checklist

Dress code

  • Smart blazer or smart top in neutral colour (navy, white, beige)
  • Hair neat and pulled back from face
  • Minimal, elegant jewellery — small earrings, watch OK
  • Light, natural makeup — looks professional on camera
  • Avoid : visible logos, distracting patterns, hoodies, t-shirts

Tech setup

  • Laptop with working camera and microphone — test 1 hour before
  • Wired internet if possible, or strong WiFi — close to router
  • Quiet room, door closed, "do not disturb" sign on door
  • Lighting : face the window, not your back to it
  • Background : neutral wall, no laundry, no distractions
  • Laptop charger plugged in
  • ID/passport visible on the desk
  • Glass of water within reach
  • Phone on silent, notifications off everywhere
  • Tab with Hotelschool platform open as backup
  • Backup : phone hotspot ready in case WiFi fails

Mental prep

  • 15 min before : breathing 4-4-6 x 5 times
  • 10 min before : review pitch + closing statement out loud
  • 5 min before : smile in the mirror for 30 seconds (literally)
  • 2 min before : open Teams, sit down, posture straight
  • During : look at camera (not screen), pause briefly between answers, smile
  • If you don't know : "That's a great question. I haven't thought about it in that way before, but my instinct would be..."

The mindset

Remember : They've already invited you to the interview. They liked your application, your essay, your test results. The interview is not a test you can fail — it's a conversation. They want to discover what you're like in person.

Your mantra : "I belong here. I have something to bring."
